Opening

Who Was Mother Teresa, Really?

This session is built around a simple method: introduce the key theme briefly, then let Mother Teresa answer in her own words through recorded interviews.

  • Not merely a symbol of kindness, but a radical disciple.
  • Deeply loved, widely criticized, and often misunderstood.
  • Uniquely relevant because we can still hear her directly.
“We will let her speak for herself.”
Suggested speaking time: 3–4 minutes
Session arc

Flow for the hour

  • Life and calling
  • Core teachings
  • Criticisms and responses
  • Spiritual darkness and perseverance
  • Application for Catholic men today

Life and calling

Brief Timeline

1910Born in Skopje to an Albanian family.
1928–1929Joins the Sisters of Loreto and travels to India.
1946Receives the “call within a call.”
1948Leaves the security of the convent to enter the slums.
1950Founding of the Missionaries of Charity.
1979Receives the Nobel Peace Prize.
1997Dies in Kolkata.
Suggested speaking time: 4 minutes

Why this matters

  • Her holiness unfolded in the modern world, not in a distant century.
  • Her mission grew from obedience, not institutional strategy.
  • The timeline helps frame later questions about her methods and motives.

The Radical Leap

  • Many thought this was reckless.
  • For her, it was obedience.
  • This became one of the clearest signs of divine providence in her life.

Core teaching

Love That Hurts

For Mother Teresa, love was never sentimental. True love costs something. It takes flesh in sacrifice, inconvenience, and self-gift.

“If we really want to love God, we must give until it hurts.”

Core teaching

Sharing, Not Merely Donating

Her message goes beyond charitable giving. She wanted people to come close enough to the poor that love became personal.

  • Donating can remain distant.
  • Sharing is relational and sacrificial.
  • The poor are not a cause; they are our brothers and sisters.
Suggested speaking time: 2–3 minutes

Possible transition

Move from this slide into the next by showing that poverty is not only material. She believed the West also suffers a devastating poverty—the hunger to be loved.

Controversies

Why the Criticisms Exist

Many objections to Mother Teresa come from a deeper clash of worldviews.

  • Material efficiency versus spiritual meaning
  • Systemic analysis versus person-to-person love
  • Secular moral assumptions versus Catholic convictions about life, suffering, and sexuality
A fair treatment of criticism begins by understanding the lens through which it is made.

Use a calm tone here

Present criticisms briefly and honestly. Then move quickly to her own answers. This prevents the session from becoming reactive or defensive.

Deepest lesson

Spiritual Darkness and Unwavering Faith

One of the most powerful dimensions of her life was hidden for years: a prolonged interior darkness, and yet total perseverance in service.

  • She did not live by consolations.
  • She lived by fidelity.
  • This may be the most relevant lesson for men carrying unseen burdens.
Faith is not the same as emotional certainty.

Presenter note

You can read a short quotation from her letters here, or simply explain that sainthood often includes dryness, silence, and perseverance without visible consolation.

Closing application

What Does This Mean for Us?

  • Love your family first.
  • See loneliness as real poverty.
  • Share, do not merely donate.
  • Defend life concretely, not abstractly.
  • Do not wait for perfect clarity before obeying God.
  • Accept that fidelity may include darkness.
  • Start with one person, one act, one sacrifice.
“Where is God asking me to step out without guarantees?”

Closing suggestion

End with one minute of silence, a short prayer, or a question for table discussion.
